## 02 · Onboarding in six steps
phase: ABSORB
duration: 12

SAY:
When you onboard a new hire, you do six things. First — here's who I am and how I work. That's Bio. Second — here's what you're allowed to do and what's off limits. That's Rules. Third — here's how to sound when you represent me. That's Personas. Fourth — here's how to do specific jobs, step by step. That's Skills. Fifth — here's your daily schedule, the rhythm of the work. That's Automations. Sixth — here's the filing cabinet, the reference material, everything you need to pull from. That's Files. You never have to memorize what each primitive does. Just ask: what would I tell a new employee? The answer maps directly to a primitive.

## 03 · Why this mental model matters
phase: ABSORB
duration: 8

SAY:
This isn't just an analogy. It's a design principle. When your agent does something weird — talks too formally, misses a boundary, doesn't know your kids' names — ask yourself: did I onboard this properly? Would a new employee know this? If the answer is no, you know exactly which primitive to update. Agent's too wordy? Adjust the Persona. Sent an email without asking? Add a Rule. Doesn't know your schedule? Update the Bio. Every problem maps to an onboarding gap. Every fix is a primitive update.

## 04 · What makes it agentic — task loops
phase: ABSORB
duration: 10

SAY:
Here's the key distinction. A chatbot waits for you to talk to it. An agent has task loops — recurring work that runs on a rhythm. There are three types. On-demand — you ask, it does. Brain dump, content draft, meeting prep. These are skills you invoke when you need them. Triggered — something happens, and the agent responds. An email from a VIP lands, the agent flags it. A deadline approaches, the agent alerts you. Scheduled — the agent acts on a clock. Morning briefing at seven thirty. Follow-up tracker at five. Weekly reflection on Sunday. Scheduled task loops are what separate an agent from a chatbot. The chatbot is reactive. The agent is proactive.

## 05 · The employee test
phase: ABSORB
duration: 8

SAY:
Here's a test you can run for any agent setup. Imagine you hired a real person for this job. Would they have enough information to start working on day one? If your Bio is empty, they don't know who you are. If your Rules are missing, they'll make assumptions about what's okay. If your Skills are vague, they'll improvise instead of following a process. If your Automations aren't set, they'll just sit there waiting for you to assign work. Every gap in your configuration is a gap in your employee's onboarding. Fill the gaps, and the agent performs.

## 06 · Jobs to be done, not tasks
phase: ABSORB
duration: 10

SAY:
One more distinction that matters. Don't think in tasks. Think in jobs to be done. A task is "check my email." A job to be done is "every morning, triage my inbox — separate VIP messages from action items from FYI, flag anything urgent, and send me a summary before I check my phone. Definition of done: SMS delivered, digest saved." See the difference? A task is a single action. A job has a trigger, steps, and a clear definition of done. When you write Skills for your agent, write jobs — not tasks. The definition of done is what makes it agentic. The agent knows when the work is finished. It doesn't need you to say "good, now do the next thing."

## 07 · Definition of done
phase: ABSORB
duration: 8

SAY:
This is the most important concept in the whole course. Every skill, every automation, every job your agent does needs a definition of done. Not "send me something useful." Not "check on things." Specific. Measurable. "SMS delivered with today's top three priorities. Saved to Digests folder." "Brain dump organized into four categories. Urgent next actions sent via SMS." "Weekly reflection emailed. Includes wins, patterns, and next week's top three." When the definition of done is clear, the agent can work independently. When it's vague, the agent guesses. And guessing is not agentic.

## 08 · Three areas of your life
phase: ABSORB
duration: 9

SAY:
When you build your Chief of Staff, you'll define jobs across three areas. Household — the logistics of running a home. Morning briefing with calendar and family schedule. Meal planning. Errand tracking. Personal — the things that keep you grounded. Morning intention. Brain dumps when your head is full. Weekly reflection so you see what you accomplished, not just what's left. Business — the operational grind. Client follow-ups. Content drafting in your voice. Evening wind-down so you can actually close your laptop and be done. Three areas. Nine jobs. All with clear definitions of done.
